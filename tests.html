Handcoding a responsive website

This project was my first attempt at prototyping, coding and testing a responsive website for various viewports and devices. The tools were Adobe XD, CSS media queries and breakpoints in combination with CSS grid layouts.



This exercise introduced me to the design and implementation of multi-device layout patterns for responsive webdesign. I now feel quite confident coding responsive grid layouts with CSS media queries.

The task was to design a website about an art style, but also to incorporate the art style into the design of the website itself. I was given the art style 'post-modernism', however, this style is incredibly broad. I therefore narrowed it down to the Memphis Movement founded in Milan in the 1980s. My first step was to research the Memphis style and then develop a moodboard to serve as a guideline and inspiration. I defined rational and emotional keywords, as well colors, typography and textures. I made sure to keep the moodboard visible throughout the whole process.

Part of the exercise was also learning about Design Principles such a Gestalt principles and different types of contrasts. Inspired by the Memphis art movement, I incorporated texture contrasts, orientation contrasts and color contrasts into the design of the site, which can be seen in the graphics of the background image which I designed in Illustrator. I also applied Gestalt principles such as figure-ground (the CSS grid areas are placed on a fixed background adding dimension), closure, proximity and symmetry.

The next step was to design a multidevice layout pattern which would work on desktops, tablets and phones. I went for the "mostly fluid" layout type:

"A multi-column layout that introduces larger margins on big screens, relies on fluid grids and images to scale from large screens down to small screen sizes, and stacks columns vertically in its narrowest incarnations‚Äù

I then went on to sketch wireframes on paper for each device type.

After sketching basic wireframes on paper, it was time to create my first high fidelity prototype in Adobe XD. To ensure coherent visual identity on all the pages, I developed a style tile defining the typography, color scheme, and button layout.

After building the high fidelity prototype, it was time to user-test it.
I chose to conduct a Five Second test of the landing page shared on UsabilityHub and I also carried out face-to-face interviews.
I wanted to measure the first impression given by the landing page, as users will bounce off a site in a matter of seconds if you fail to grab their attention fast. I wanted to test what participants thought the page was about, after viewing it for just five seconds.
The test consisted of three questions with test group size of 10, of which 7 participated. The questions were the following:

- What do you think the website was about?
- What was the purpose of the website?
- What was the most prominent element on the page?

Test findings: My test results showed that it was not clear to the users that the page was about an art style. Some thought it was a commercial website selling high-end design furniture or a page about interior design. My planned changes were therefore to reiterate the design and change the background image.
